Restaurant UI Redesigned

Improving a self-ordering interface with Nielsen's 10 usability heuristics

This case study focused on identifying friction in a restaurant self-ordering flow and redesigning the interface to feel clearer, more predictable, and less error-prone. The work centered on usability problems that interrupted ordering confidence, especially around pricing, category navigation, and system feedback.

Key Usability Problems

Pricing clarity and consistency

Unclear pricing, including the confusing "0 kr" state, made it harder for users to understand what they were paying for and when extra costs applied.

Navigation that caused detours

Users had to tap categories repeatedly or ended up in the wrong section, which broke flow and increased frustration.

Side-scrolling made discovery harder

The category bar required unnecessary scanning and slowed down access to the area users actually wanted.

Weak error feedback

When something failed in the backend, users were left without enough explanation to recover confidently.

Design Process

The redesign used heuristic evaluation as the main structure for identifying and prioritizing issues. The goal was not just to make the interface look better, but to remove hesitation points and make the ordering flow easier to understand on the first try.

  • Visibility of system status: Improved progress and feedback so users can tell what the interface is doing.
  • Recognition over recall: Reworked navigation so categories are easier to spot without extra memory load.
  • Error prevention and recovery: Added clearer messaging to help users understand what went wrong and what to do next.
  • Aesthetic and minimalist design: Reduced clutter so the ordering choices are easier to scan.

What Changed

The redesign aimed to make the interface easier to learn and easier to recover from when something goes wrong.

  • Pricing became more transparent by showing base prices and additional size costs more consistently.
  • Category navigation became more static and visible, reducing unnecessary taps and scrolling.
  • Error states became easier to understand with clearer messages and recovery guidance.
  • Dismissible feedback replaced more restrictive timing behavior, giving users more control.
  • Progress cues such as scroll indicators improved awareness of where users were in the menu.

Outcome

This redesign shows how heuristic-based iteration can improve more than just surface visuals. By clarifying system feedback, reducing recall demands, and simplifying navigation, the interface became easier to trust and easier to use efficiently.
