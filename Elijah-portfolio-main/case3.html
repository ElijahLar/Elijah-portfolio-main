Scent-Driven Storytelling

Designing an olfactory journaling experience for deeper reflection

This project explored whether scent could act as a softer, more atmospheric journaling prompt than written questions. Instead of directing users with text, the concept used curated scent cues to help people ease into reflection, access memory more organically, and develop stronger narrative flow while writing.

The Design Opportunity

Many journaling tools depend on visual prompts or written instructions. Those can be useful, but they can also feel effortful or overly directive. Because smell is strongly connected to emotion and autobiographical memory, we saw an opportunity to design an experience that supported reflection more indirectly and more personally.

Why scent?

Scent can trigger memory and emotion in a way that feels immediate and personal, making it a promising prompt for reflective writing.

Designing for reflection

The experience needed to feel calm and open-ended rather than task-driven, because reflective writing is easy to interrupt.

Balancing physical and digital interaction

The interface had to support scent selection without letting the digital layer overpower the tactile journaling ritual.

Process and Prototyping

The project treated scent as an interaction element instead of a decorative add-on. Early prototypes explored simple delivery methods such as jars and cotton swabs. Later iterations moved toward Arduino-controlled diffusion to reduce friction and create a more seamless transition into writing.

  • Different scent blends were developed for different moods and times of day.
  • Manual delivery methods were tested before moving toward automated diffusion.
  • The final concept combined a physical journaling box, scent hardware, and a lightweight mobile interface.

Testing and Findings

The prototype was evaluated through a small exploratory study with six participants. Some journaled with scent cues while others journaled without them, which allowed the team to compare the experience across both conditions.

  • Narrative flow: Participants often entered writing flow faster with scent-supported journaling.
  • Emotional depth: Entries tended to contain richer emotional language and more detailed memory recall.
  • Ritual and transition: The scent cue helped mark the beginning of reflective time and created a clearer mental shift into journaling.

Reflection

One of the clearest lessons was that even small interruptions can weaken reflective experiences. Manual scent handling introduced just enough friction to pull attention away from writing, which made the move toward automation especially valuable. The project reinforced how interaction design can shape mood, attention, and emotional engagement, not just task completion.
